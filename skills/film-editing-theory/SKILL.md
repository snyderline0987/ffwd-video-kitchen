# Film Editing Theory — Reference for Editing Agents

This skill is a **decision reference**, not an executor. It does not cut video. It tells the agent **why** to cut, **where** to cut, and **how long** to hold a shot.

Load it whenever an agent is about to make or evaluate a cut, build a montage sequence, set scene timing, or decide between alternative takes.

## When to consult this skill

| Situation | Primary framework |
|---|---|
| Should I cut here at all? | Dmytryk #1 |
| Exactly which frame? | Murch (Rule of Six) |
| Trim long or short? | Dmytryk #2 → long |
| Continuity is broken — keep the cut? | Murch #1 + Dmytryk #6 |
| Building a hook / viral short / fast intro | Eisenstein: metric + rhythmic |
| Idea-driven thematic sequence | Eisenstein: intellectual montage |
| Dialogue or interview pacing | Tarantino/Menke #3, #5, #7 |
| Genre-aware / citation-heavy edit | Tarantino/Menke #8 |
| Are jump cuts OK here? | Yes if deliberate (Dmytryk #9) AND emotion-justified (Murch #1) |

## Conflict resolution — the priority chain

When two theses point in opposite directions, resolve in this order:

1. **Emotion wins** — Murch's 51% rule is the top of every hierarchy.
2. **Substance before form** — Dmytryk #7. What is being said > how it's edited.
3. **Conflict creates meaning** — Eisenstein. If a "smooth" cut neutralizes the dialectic, choose the rougher one.
4. **Propel the scene** — Menke. Forward motion beats technical correctness.

## 1. Walter Murch — *In the Blink of an Eye* (1995, expanded 2001)

Editor of *Apocalypse Now*, *The Godfather III*, *The English Patient* (Oscar for both editing and sound), *The Conversation*. His "Rule of Six" is the de-facto industry standard for evaluating any cut.

1. **Emotion is paramount (51%).** Does the cut reflect what the audience should be feeling right now? If a cut damages the emotion, it is wrong — no matter how technically clean.
2. **Story (23%).** Does the cut advance the narrative? A beautiful cut that doesn't move the story is a wasted cut.
3. **Rhythm (10%).** Does the cut land at a rhythmically right moment? Internal tempo is real even without music.
4. **Eye-trace (7%).** Honor where the audience's eye is already looking inside the frame. Cut so the new shot's focal point lands where the eye was already prepared to go.
5. **2D plane / 180° rule (5%).** Respect screen direction. Break it only on purpose.
6. **3D spatial continuity (4%).** Physical/spatial consistency. Lowest priority — most disposable.
7. **Sacrifice from the bottom up.** When forced to violate one criterion, give up 3D first, then 2D, then rhythm, eye-trace, story. Never sacrifice emotion.
8. **Cut where the audience would blink.** A blink is the brain accepting a thought-change. The right cut point is where a thoughtful viewer would naturally blink.
9. **Sound and picture are asymmetric.** Hearing is continuous; sight is not. Use J-cuts and L-cuts; let audio lead picture or trail it.
10. **Distance editing.** Step away from the timeline for hours or days before locking. Familiarity hides flaws that fresh eyes catch instantly.

## 2. Edward Dmytryk — *On Film Editing* (1984)

Director of 50+ features. His seven rules (here expanded with three derivative principles to ten) are the structural baseline below Murch's emotional layer.

1. **Never cut without a positive reason.** Stay on a shot as long as it's the best shot for delivering the required information. Boredom is not a reason; "improvement" is.
2. **When undecided, cut long, not short.** It is always cheaper to trim than to recover frames.
3. **Cut in movement.** Hide the cut inside an action; motion in both shots masks the splice.
4. **Fresh is preferable to stale.** When a frame is exhausted, change it. If you need to pad, pad at the *head* of the new shot, never the tail of the old one.
5. **Scenes begin and end with continuing action.** Enter and exit on motion to maintain narrative momentum.
6. **Cut for proper values, not proper matches.** Continuity errors die in the audience's emotion. A mismatch the audience doesn't notice is not a mismatch.
7. **Substance first, then form.** What is being said beats how it's edited.
8. **First viewing should evoke emotion, not analysis.** If a viewer admires your technique, your technique has failed.
9. **Properly made cuts are invisible.** Visible cuts (jump cuts, smash cuts) must be deliberate stylistic choices, never accidents.
10. **Picture leads, sound accompanies.** Except in musical sequences, design the audio *around* the image, not the other way around.

## 3. Sergei Eisenstein — *Film Form* / Montage Theory (1920s–40s)

Eisenstein treated cinema as dialectical: meaning emerges from the **collision** of shots, not their smooth continuity. This framework is essential for montage sequences, intros, ideological/persuasive content, music-driven cutting, and short-form viral video — TikTok and Reels are structurally Eisensteinian (metric montage at industrial scale).

1. **Shots are cells, not bricks.** Every shot has internal energy. A cut is mitosis — division and growth — not stacking.
2. **Conflict creates meaning.** Between shot scales, framing, light/dark, tempo, motion vectors, sound. Without conflict between adjacent shots, there is no montage.
3. **Metric montage.** Cut on fixed frame/beat counts. Build tension by progressive shortening of pieces. The mechanics behind every modern fast-cut hook.
4. **Rhythmic montage.** Cut length is driven by content *within* the frame, not by an external meter. Action cuts to action.
5. **Tonal montage.** Cuts driven by the emotional/lighting/sonic tone of each shot. The shot's "feel" decides the cut, not its action.
6. **Overtonal montage.** Synthesis of metric + rhythmic + tonal operating simultaneously. The mature mode.
7. **Intellectual montage.** Two shots juxtaposed to produce a *concept* neither shot contains alone (Eisenstein's striking workers + slaughtered bull = "the masses are being butchered"). The most powerful and most easily abused mode.
8. **The Kuleshov effect.** Meaning lives in the cut, not in the shot. The same neutral face reads as hunger, grief, or desire depending on the shot beside it. Plan adjacencies, not isolated frames.
9. **A shot is an ambiguous hieroglyph.** A shot only resolves in context. Always plan in pairs and triplets, never singles.
10. **Montage is the nerve of cinema.** When there is nothing to cut to, there is no film. Always source/shoot enough collision material before sitting down to edit.

## 4. Quentin Tarantino & Sally Menke — Modern Collaborative Editing

Sally Menke cut every Tarantino film from *Reservoir Dogs* (1992) through *Inglourious Basterds* (2009) until her death in 2010. Their partnership is the most documented modern director-editor working method and applies directly to genre-aware, dialogue-heavy, remix-style, and channel-driven content (including short-form YouTube and Reels).

1. **Every scene is a mini-film.** Beginning, middle, end. A scene must work on its own — pulled out, watched as a clip on YouTube — before it works in the whole. Critical for any channel where individual scenes will be reposted.
2. **Editing is rewriting.** Tarantino: *"The last draft of the screenplay is the first cut of the movie. The final cut of the movie is the last draft of the script."* The edit is authorship, not assembly. An editor (or an editing agent) is allowed to restructure.
3. **First pass without music.** Build emotional and dramatic truth on dialogue and image alone. Lay music *after*, then re-tweak cuts to the beats. Reverse this order at your peril — music will paper over weak edits and you'll never see them.
4. **Genre immersion before cutting.** Menke watched 30–50 reference films before *Kill Bill*. Pipeline equivalent: feed the agent reference clips and style examples *before* the edit decisions, not during.
5. **Watch the body, not just the mouth.** Cut on mannerisms, gestures, micro-reactions, weight shifts. The body speaks earlier and truer than dialogue. Reaction shots beat action shots.
6. **Audiences tolerate discontinuity.** Do not worship match cuts. If the cut propels the scene, broken continuity is invisible. Menke routinely broke the 180° rule on purpose.
7. **Long takes are a tool, not a sin.** A scene that breathes earns its punchline or its violence. Never cut just because something has been on screen "too long" — see Dmytryk #1.
8. **Embrace iconography.** Don't hide references. In a contemporary, citation-aware audience, visible genre awareness reads as *authorship*, not derivativeness. (Especially relevant for short-form formats that quote/remix.)
9. **Director-editor trust over rules.** Intuit the tone first, defend it in argument (not debate), change it the moment the director's instinct out-reads yours. For agent pipelines: respect the channel/creator's documented voice over generic best-practice.
10. **Internal rhythm is signature.** Every editor has an innate response to footage that becomes their fingerprint. Honor it. For AI pipelines: define and hold a **house rhythm** per channel or brand — measurable parameters (avg shot length, cut-to-music-beat ratio, reaction-frame count) that make the output recognizable as *yours* and not interchangeable.

## How an editing agent should use this skill

When called during a cut decision, the agent should:

1. **State the decision.** ("Should I cut at frame 412? How long should this shot hold?")
2. **Identify the framework** from the matrix at the top.
3. **Apply the relevant theses by number**, briefly stating the reasoning.
4. **If theses conflict**, follow the priority chain (Emotion → Substance → Conflict → Forward Motion).
5. **Cite the thesis number(s) used** in the agent's reasoning log so cuts are traceable and reviewable.

Default behavior under uncertainty: **hold longer (Dmytryk #2), serve emotion (Murch #1), keep the scene moving forward (Menke #6).**
